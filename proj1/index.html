Project 1: Images of the Russian Empire

Overview

This project uses computer vision techniques to colorize early Prokudin-Gorskii glass plate photographs. Each scene was captured as three vertically stacked grayscale images (B, G, R channels). I separated and aligned them to reconstruct full-color images and explored how pyramid-based alignment improves results on larger images.

Problem

Given a single grayscale plate with three stacked channel exposures, align the channels so that corresponding scene features (edges, corners, textures) overlap correctly. Misalignment produces color fringing; the task is to find integer (or subpixel) shifts per channel that minimize misalignment error.

Part 1: Naive Approach — Iterative

I converted each plate into three channel images and tried exhaustive search over integer pixel shifts (Δx, Δy) to align G and B to R. I compared L2 distance and normalized cross-correlation (NCC). For small images, NCC was more stable. Cropping ~10% off borders before scoring reduced edge artifacts and improved the alignment estimates.

Part 2: Improved Approach — Image Pyramid

For larger plates, exhaustive search becomes slow and less robust. I built an image pyramid: align coarser, downsampled versions first to get a rough shift, then refine at progressively higher resolutions. This approach significantly reduced compute while improving overall alignment, especially on high-resolution images.
